Mohammad Sami Alsharef
English - The Danger of a Single Story
Answers to listening/comprehension questions

Video: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=D9Ihs241zeg

---

**Q1 - Answer: B**

The main massage of the talk is that when you only hear one story about
a group of people or a place, you end up with wrong and incomplete
ideas about them. She wants to warn us that this leads to stereotypes
and misunderstandings. It's basically saying we need more then one story.

---

**Q2 - Answer: B**

She starts by saying "I'm a storyteller" and right away shares a personal
anecdote from her childhood about how she wrote stories with white characters
even though she lived in Nigeria. Its a good hook because it's personal and
makes you curious where she's going with it.

---

**Q3 - Answer: B**

After talking about her british and american books she shifts to saying
how "impressionable and vulnerable we are" when it comes to stories.
Then she explains how discovering african writers like Chinua Achebe
completly changed how she saw literature - she realised people like her
could exist in books too.

---

**Q4 - Answer: B**

First she talks about Fide, her family's house boy. She only knew one thing
about him - that his family was poor. Then she was shocked to see that his
brother made a beautiful basket, because her single story of them was just
"they are poor." She then connects this to her american roommate who had
the same problem but about all of Africa - the roomate's single story was
basically "Africa = catastrophe."

---

**Q5 - Answer: A**

At the end she says that when we reject the single story and accept that
there's never just one story about any place, we "regain a kind of paradise."
So her final point is really hopefull - it's not saying stories are bad,
it's saying more stories = better understanding.

---

**Q6 - Answer: B**

The basket moment is when she realised her single story about Fide's family
(that they were only poor, nothing else) was blinding her. She couldn't imagine
they could make something beautiful. This perfectly shows what a single story
does - it makes you unable to see any other side of a person or group.

---

**Q7 - Answer: B**

She transitions by saying something like "if I hadn't grown up in Nigeria,
and all I knew was from popular images, I too would think Africa was a place
of beautiful landscapes...waiting to be saved by a white foreigner."
Then she goes back in history and brings up John Lok from 1561 who wrote
about africans as "beasts with no houses." That's where the single story tradition started.

---

**Q8 - Answer: B**

She explains that single stories don't just happen randomly. They happen
because of power - who gets to tell the story and who doesn't. She introduces
the igbo word "nkali" which means something like "being greater than another."
So the connection is: whoever has power, controls the narrative.

---

**Q9 - Answer: A**

She mentions Achebe and Camara Laye because they where the ones that shifted
her thinking. When she read their books she realised that "people like me"
girls with chocolate skin and kinky hair, could also be in literature.
They basically saved her from having a single story about what books are.

---

**Q10 - Answer: B**

She went to Guadalajara and expected it to be like what american media showed -
mexicans as problem immigrants. But she saw real people going to work, rolling
tortillas, laughing. She felt ashamed because she realised she had been
believing the single story of Mexicans as "abject immigrants." She admits
that she herself was guilty of the same thing she critisices others for.

---

**Q11 - Answer: C**

She tells this story as a sarcastic point. A student said its "such a shame
that Nigerian men are abusers" based on a character in her novel. So she
replied - well I read American Psycho and its such a shame all young americans
are serial killers. Obviously she doesn't believe that. The point is we would
never generalise all Americans from one novel's character, but we do it to
Africans - because America has cultural power and "many stories."

---

**Q12 - Answer: A**

She talks about having a happy childhood with love and laughter, but ALSO
about grandfathers dying in refugee camps, a friend dying in a plane crash
because there was no water in fire trucks, and jam/bread slowly disappearing
from the breakfast table under military goverments. She shows both sides to
prove no single narrative can define who she is. Her identity is all of these.

---

**Q13 - Answer: B**

A professor told her her novel wasn't "authentically African" because her
characters drove cars and where educated - they where not starving. She
completely rejects this idea. She didn't even know what "African authenticity"
was suppost to mean. It's another example of a single story - the idea that
africans must be portrayed as poor to be "real."

---

**Q14 - Answer: C**

She tells the story of a woman who worked as a messenger at a TV station
in Lagos. This woman had read her novel, didn't like the ending, and told
Chimamanda exactly what she should write in the sequel. The point is that
regular Nigerians DO read - the assumption that they don't is just another
single story.

---

**Q15 - Answer: A**

She doesn't attack the audience or blame them. Instead she uses her own
examples - her biased roommate, or herself with the Mexicans - to show
that anyone can fall into the single story trap. This makes the audience
reflect on their own biases without feeling judged directly. It's a clever
way to get the message accross.

---

**Q16 - Answer: B**

The main idea about power is that whoever has cultural and economic power
gets to shape the dominant story. The West had the power to write about
Africa for centuries, and because of that their version became the "official"
story. Other perspectives where left out. So language and culture are deeply
connected to power - you can't separate them.

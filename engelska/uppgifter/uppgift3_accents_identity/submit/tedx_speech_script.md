Honourable Members and distinguished guests, thank you for inviting me to this TEDx session at the European Parliament. My topic is Accents and Identity. In a multilingual Union that often uses English, the way we speak reveals our heritage and shapes how we are perceived. I will make three points: first, accents should be celebrated as part of our identity; second, bias against accents has real consequences; and third, we can promote inclusive communication that values accent diversity.

---

When adults learn English, it is natural for traces of their mother tongue to remain. The British Council notes that learners "don't need a perfect English accent to be able to communicate fluently". A distinctive accent can even be an ice-breaker and shows that someone has worked hard to master another language. The same article reminds us that there are "thousands and thousands of accents" among native speakers, and most English speakers worldwide are non-native. So-called "standard" accents are actually in the minority. Our first point, therefore, is that accents are an intrinsic part of who we are and should be worn with pride.

---

Unfortunately, accents can trigger prejudice. A Horizon Magazine report highlights growing evidence that people with non-native accents are perceived as less educated or less trustworthy and may find it harder to get a job. Psycholinguist Dr Alice Foucart's research found that listening to a foreign accent requires more cognitive effort, which can lead to negative judgments. In experiments with Flemish listeners, native speakers were rated as more intelligent and successful than foreign speakers. With over 250 million people living and working outside their home countries, accent bias affects hiring decisions, court proceedings and classroom interactions across Europe. Recognising that such bias exists is the first step toward addressing it.

---

How can we promote inclusion? Comedian Trevor Noah once said, "An accent is not a measure of intelligence. An accent is just somebody speaking your language with the rules of theirs". This phrase reminds us that accents reflect the rules of another language, not a lack of ability. Institutions — including this Parliament — should acknowledge accent bias in diversity policies and train staff to listen without prejudice. Employers and universities can run accent-awareness workshops. Research shows that simply hearing a variety of voices reduces bias. Educators should focus on clarity and intelligibility rather than insisting on a "neutral" accent. Exposing students to a range of speakers — Italians, Swedes, Nigerians or Indians — demonstrates there is no single correct way to sound. Celebrating successful people who retain their accents reinforces this message. Lastly, each of us can practise patience: when you hear an unfamiliar accent, listen attentively and ask clarifying questions instead of judging.

---

Accents sit at the intersection of identity and perception. They enrich our conversations and tell our stories, yet they can also become grounds for unfair judgments. By celebrating accent diversity, understanding the cognitive reasons behind accent bias and adopting inclusive practices, we can turn the melody of our voices into a source of strength. Let us commit to hearing the meaning behind the accent and help build a more inclusive and linguistically rich Europe.
